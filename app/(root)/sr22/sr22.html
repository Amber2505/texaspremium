SR-22 Insurance in Texas: Everything You Need to Know

Need an SR-22 in Texas? This guide explains what an SR-22 is, why you might need it, how to get it, and what it means for Texas drivers looking to reinstate their driving privileges.

What Is SR-22 Insurance in Texas?

In Texas, an SR-22 is a Certificate of Financial Responsibility filed by your auto insurance company with the Texas Department of Public Safety (DPS) to prove you carry the state’s minimum liability insurance coverage. It’s not a type of insurance but a document verifying that you meet Texas’s financial responsibility requirements, typically required for high-risk drivers after serious driving violations or license suspensions.

The SR-22 ensures you maintain continuous insurance coverage for a specified period, allowing you to regain or maintain your driving privileges in Texas.

Why Might You Need an SR-22 in Texas?

In Texas, an SR-22 is required for drivers who have committed certain violations or had their license suspended. Common reasons include:

  • DWI or DUI convictions: Driving while intoxicated or under the influence is a common trigger for an SR-22 requirement.
  • Driving without insurance: Operating a vehicle without valid liability insurance violates Texas law and often requires an SR-22.
  • Driving with a suspended or revoked license: If caught driving while your license is suspended, you may need an SR-22 to reinstate it.
  • Serious traffic violations: Offenses like reckless driving or accumulating too many points on your driving record can lead to an SR-22 mandate.
  • At-fault accidents without insurance: Causing an accident while uninsured often requires an SR-22 to prove future financial responsibility.
  • Failure to pay judgments: Unresolved traffic-related judgments may necessitate an SR-22 filing.

The Texas DPS or a court will notify you if an SR-22 is required, typically through a court order or a letter from the DPS.

How Does SR-22 Insurance Work in Texas?

An SR-22 in Texas is a form your insurance provider files with the Texas DPS to confirm you have at least the state’s minimum liability insurance, which is 30/60/25 coverage ($30,000 per person, $60,000 per accident for bodily injury, and $25,000 for property damage). Here’s how the process works:

  1. Notification: You receive a court order or DPS letter stating you need an SR-22.
  2. Contact Your Insurer: If you have an active auto insurance policy, ask your provider to file an SR-22. If you don’t have insurance or your insurer doesn’t offer SR-22 filings, you’ll need to purchase a policy from a provider that does.
  3. Filing the SR-22: Your insurer files the SR-22 form with the Texas DPS, usually electronically, for a small fee.
  4. Maintain Coverage: You must keep continuous insurance coverage for the duration of the SR-22 requirement, typically 2 years in Texas.
  5. DPS Monitoring: The DPS monitors your insurance status. If your policy lapses or is canceled, your insurer files an SR-26 form to notify the DPS, which may lead to license suspension or other penalties.
  6. Completion: After the SR-22 period ends, you can request your insurer to stop filing the SR-22, potentially reducing your premiums if they increased due to your high-risk status.

Types of SR-22 Certificates in Texas

Texas offers three types of SR-22 certificates, depending on your situation:

  • Operator’s Certificate (Non-Owner SR-22): For drivers who don’t own a vehicle but need insurance to drive borrowed or rented cars.
  • Owner’s Certificate: For drivers who own and insure their own vehicle(s).
  • Owner-Operator Certificate: Covers drivers who own a vehicle and also drive non-owned vehicles, providing flexibility.

How Much Does SR-22 Insurance Cost in Texas?

The SR-22 filing fee in Texas is typically $15–$25, a one-time or per-term charge from your insurer. However, because SR-22 drivers are considered high-risk, your auto insurance premiums will likely be higher than standard rates. Costs vary based on the violation, driving history, and insurer, but here are some averages:

  • Full Coverage with SR-22: After a DWI, full coverage in Texas averages $2,800–$3,500 per year, compared to $2,278 for standard full coverage.
  • Minimum Liability with SR-22: A liability-only policy with an SR-22 after a DWI averages $1,200–$1,800 annually.
  • Violation Impact: A DWI can increase premiums by $800–$1,200 per year, while driving without insurance may result in a smaller increase.

Insurers like Progressive, GEICO, or Texas-based providers like Germania Insurance may offer competitive SR-22 rates. Comparing quotes from multiple providers is the best way to find affordable coverage.

How Long Do You Need an SR-22 in Texas?

In Texas, the SR-22 requirement typically lasts 2 years from the date of your conviction or judgment, though this can vary based on the offense. For example:

  • DWI: Usually requires an SR-22 for 2 years, starting from the conviction date.
  • Driving Without Insurance: Often 2 years, starting from the date of license reinstatement.

If your insurance lapses during this period, the DPS will be notified via an SR-26 form, potentially leading to license suspension and restarting the 2-year requirement. Contact the Texas DPS to confirm your specific end date.

How to Get an SR-22 in Texas

To obtain an SR-22 in Texas:

  1. Check with Your Insurer: If you have an active policy, ask your provider if they file SR-22s. Many major insurers in Texas, like State Farm, Allstate, or Progressive, offer this service.
  2. Shop for a Policy: If you don’t have insurance or your insurer doesn’t file SR-22s, contact providers specializing in high-risk drivers. Independent insurance agents can help compare options.
  3. Non-Owner SR-22: If you don’t own a car, a non-owner SR-22 policy can meet Texas requirements for driving borrowed or rented vehicles.
  4. Pay the Filing Fee: Expect a $15–$25 fee, which may be charged upfront or per policy term.
  5. Maintain Coverage: Ensure your policy stays active to avoid DPS penalties.

Resources like The Zebra or local Texas insurance agents can simplify the process of finding SR-22 coverage.

Consequences of Not Maintaining an SR-22 in Texas

If your insurance lapses or is canceled while an SR-22 is required:

  • Your insurer will file an SR-26 form to notify the Texas DPS.
  • Your driver’s license and vehicle registration may be suspended.
  • You may face fines, reinstatement fees (up to $100), or an extended SR-22 period.
  • Driving without a valid SR-22 can lead to additional penalties, including potential jail time for repeat offenses.

To avoid these issues, set up automatic payments and renew your policy on time.

Texas-Specific Requirements and Tips

Texas has unique considerations for SR-22 filings:

  • Minimum Liability Coverage: Texas requires 30/60/25 coverage ($30,000 per person, $60,000 per accident for bodily injury, $25,000 for property damage).
  • Occupational License: If your license is suspended, you may qualify for an occupational license for limited driving (e.g., to work or school), but an SR-22 is often still required.
  • DWI Surcharges: In addition to SR-22 costs, Texas imposes surcharges for DWI convictions, ranging from $1,000–$2,000 per year for 3 years, payable to the DPS.
  • Shop Locally: Texas-based insurers like Germania or Fred Loya Insurance may offer competitive rates for SR-22 policies.
  • Avoid Lapses: Use auto-pay or calendar reminders to ensure continuous coverage, as Texas DPS is strict about lapses.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I get an SR-22 in Texas if I don’t own a car?

Yes, a non-owner SR-22 policy allows you to meet Texas requirements if you drive borrowed or rented vehicles.

How do I know when my SR-22 requirement ends in Texas?

Contact the Texas DPS or check your court order to confirm the end date, typically 2 years from conviction or reinstatement. Your insurer can stop filing the SR-22 once the requirement is fulfilled.

Does an SR-22 affect my insurance claims in Texas?

No, the SR-22 itself doesn’t impact your ability to file claims. Your policy’s coverage (e.g., liability, collision) determines what’s covered in an accident.
